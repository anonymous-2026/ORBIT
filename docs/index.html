Abstract

Vision-language-action (VLA) policies often fail not because task semantics are unavailable, but because they cannot reliably determine which objects, reference objects, spatial relations, and interaction history should govern the next action. This limitation is especially pronounced under short implicit requests such as “I bought fries,” where a policy must infer a bounded goal, ground the relevant objects, and avoid salient distractors. We introduce ORBIT, a three-stage framework that converts task semantics into action-facing control signals for VLA policies. ORBIT first expands underspecified requests into bounded task frames when necessary, constructs a task-conditioned object-relation graph, and selects a compact action-relevant subgraph. It then projects this shared subgraph into coordinated textual and visual carriers with consistency checks, before using rollout-conditioned adaptation with reward-filtered memory to select the carrier mode, relation level, and history context. We evaluate ORBIT on semantic-extension tasks, semantic-stress extensions, and benchmark-derived stress tasks that reuse benchmark scenes, objects, robot embodiments, cameras, and physics while introducing new task semantics and controlled cue perturbations. Across representative task cells, raw short-intent instructions achieve only 10-76% success, executable rewriting improves task clarity to 72-90%, and ORBIT carriers further raise success to 96-98%, outperforming rewrite-only inputs by up to 24 percentage points. Counterfactual controls show that VLA policies actively consume ORBIT's cues: wrong or corrupted relation cues reduce success by up to 70 points, while a rollout-conditioned selector reaches 93% success and avoids harmful control cues. These results suggest that reliable VLA control requires more than longer prompts or visual overlays; it requires grounded object-relation semantics delivered through validated, policy-compatible carriers.

Three-Stage Framework

ORBIT is a semantic conditioning layer over VLA policies. It avoids unconstrained prompt expansion by deriving every policy-facing cue from a selected object-relation graph and checking carrier consistency before execution.

  • Stage 1: task-conditioned graph construction and action-relevance selection.
  • Stage 2: dual-carrier projection into canonical text and visual marking.
  • Stage 3: rollout-conditioned adaptation with reward-filtered memory.

Task Setting

ORBIT uses benchmark scenes, objects, robot embodiments, cameras, and physics while introducing task semantics that expose object-relation reasoning. Semantic-extension prompts such as short food-serving or workspace-preparation requests are compared against raw instructions, executable rewrite-only inputs, validated ORBIT carriers, and harmful cue controls.

Figure 2. Prompt-to-carrier example for a short intent. The left panel shows how ORBIT decomposes “I got fries” into a bounded task frame, grounded object-relation semantics, and the final VLA-facing carrier.

Visual and Textual Carriers

ORBIT evaluates three visual semantic mix styles. IFRO places boxes, IDs, and relation marks directly in the camera views. SSP keeps camera views lighter by showing only compact IDs and moving semantic details to a side panel. DCSM combines both channels for cluttered scenes that need explicit relation and history support.

Figure 3. IFRO carrier construction for a pick-black-bowl task. ORBIT converts the original TPV/FPV inputs into VLA-facing images with in-frame object IDs, boxes, and relation markings while preserving the original scene views.

Figure 4. SSP/DCSM carrier comparison: the same ORBIT subgraph is rendered with side-panel semantics or combined in-frame relation markings.

Results

ORBIT is evaluated against executable rewrite-only inputs, not just raw short prompts. The strongest evidence comes from tasks where relation-aware carriers improve over rewrite-only, wrong or corrupted cues predictably hurt behavior, and the adaptive selector avoids harmful carrier choices.
